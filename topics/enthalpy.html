    • Enthalpy

    There are conditions that affect the amount of heat given out or absorbed from the environment in a chemical reaction.
    These include but are not limited to:

    • Temperature
    • Pressure
    • Concentration of solutions

    Enthalpy is a thermodynamic property of a system. It is the sum of the internal energy added to the product of the pressure and volume of the system. It reflects the capacity of the system to release heat.
    This means that enthalpy is the amount of energy in a thermodynamic system (enthalpy is internal energy+(pressure x volume)) It is measures in KJ/mol.

    When you measure a heat change at a constant pressure, it is called an
    enthalpy change.
    There are standard condtions necessary to measure enthalpy change these include:

    • Pressure of 100 KPa (approximately normal athmospheric pressure)
    • Temperature of 298 K (around room temperature)

    How does temperature affect enthalpy?

    When the temperature of a system increases, the kinetic and potential energies of the atoms and molecules in the system increase. Thus, the internal energy of the system increases, which means that the enthalpy of the system increases â€“ this is true under constant pressure or constant volume.
    In simple terms, If you were to increase the temperature, you would also increase the energy of the molecules, meaning those molecules interact with each other at a greater rate. Thus, you have increased your internal energy. Therefore, you would also expect your enthalpy to increase.
    Imagine increasing the temperature of water. At some point, it will turn to steam. At the critical point where the phase change occurs, there will be a decrease in temperature which will lead to a decrease in pressure. (See illustration below)

    This decrease in enthalpy occurs because te steam has lower intermolecular forces holding it together. Hence bonds can be broken with less energy.

    How does pressure affect enthalpy?

    Pressure affects the amount of heat energy given out by reactions that involve gases. As the temprature increases, so does the enthalpy and vice versa.
    This is because as pressure increases, there is more compression between molecules hence intermolecular forces between molecules increase. Thus, more energy will be needed to break bonds.
    Take for example increasing the pressure of carbon dioxide. After a while, that CO2 wil start to condense into a liquid which will have stronger forces holding its molecules together. Hence more enegy is needed to break bonds(increease in enthalpy).

    Energy level diagrams

    Energy level diagrams are diagrams used to illustrate enthalpy change by showing the energy level of the reactants and products with the vertical axis commonly representing enthalpy and the horizontal axis commonly representing the extent of the reacton. Engergy level diagrams (or Enthalpy level diagrams) usually just emphasise on a reaction when it has 100% of its reactants and when it has 0% of its reactants.
