    • Bond Enthalpies

    Bond enthalpy (also known as bond energy) is defined as the amount of energy required to break one mole of the stated bond. For example, the bond energy of a O-H single bond is 463 kJ/mol. This means that it requires 463 kJ of energy to break one mole of O-H bonds. In order to break any bond, energy must be absorbed. This lets us know that bond enthalpies are endothermic because energy is absorbed. Due to our knowledge of Hess's law, we know that the same amount of energy used to break bonds is the same amount of energy used to form them.

    Sometimes, the same bond could have slightly different enthalpies. In this case, you take the average of the given enthalpies. This is known as mean bond enthalpy.

    Using bond enthalpies to calculate energy changes

    To calculate the energy change using bond entalpies,

    • Add together the bond energies for all the bonds in the reactants - this is the 'energy in'
    • Add together the bond energies for all the bonds in the products - this is the 'energy out'
    • energy change = energy in - energy out

    Example

    Hydrogen and chlorine react to form hydrogen chloride gas:

    H−H + Cl−Cl → 2 × (H−Cl)

    Use the bond energies in the table to calculate the energy change for this reaction.

    Bond Bond Energy
    H-H 436 kJmol-1
    Cl−Cl 243 kJmol-1
    H−Cl 432 kJmol-1

    Energy in = 436 + 243 = 679 kJ mol-1

    Energy out = (2 × 432) = 864 kJ mol-1

    Energy change = in - out

    = 679 - 864

    = -185 kJ mol-1

    The energy change is negative. This shows that the reaction is exothermic.
